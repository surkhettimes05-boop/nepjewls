1. Overview & Objective

This document defines the complete brand, website/e-commerce, and marketing-advertising system for a new modern Nepalese fine jewelry brand. It is phased deliberately: a lean, fundable Phase 1 launch system, followed by Phase 2 and Phase 3 upgrades that are only justified once the business has revenue and data to support them. The intent is to avoid building luxury-conglomerate infrastructure (blockchain provenance, enterprise AR, in-house 3D-print manufacturing) before the brand has proven it can sell.

Core positioning: Shift the customer from viewing jewelry as priced bullion (gold/silver by weight) to viewing it as wearable art with a recognizable design identity and verifiable purity.

Primary market: Urban Nepal (Kathmandu Valley first), Gen Z / young millennial buyers plus traditional gifting occasions (weddings, Dashain/Tihar, engagements).

Secondary market (Phase 3+): Nepali diaspora (Gulf, US, Australia, UK) — higher willingness to pay, added shipping/customs complexity.

Success definition: A repeatable, unit-economically sound direct-to-consumer channel that does not depend on the founder's personal network to sell.

2. Brand Strategy

2.1 Positioning Statement

For young, image-conscious Nepali buyers and gifting occasions, [Brand] is the jewelry house that replaces “gold by the tola” thinking with wearable art you can verify — anchored by one recognizable motif and a guarantee of purity that Nepal's market has historically lacked.

2.2 Signature Motif (highest-priority brand asset)

A single, ownable design element repeated across every collection — the way Cartier owns the Panthère and Van Cleef & Arpels owns the Alhambra clover. This is the cheapest, highest-leverage thing in this whole PRD and should be locked before anything else ships.

Candidate directions: a stylised lotus petal edge, a geometric motif drawn from Newari temple lattice/strut work, a modern take on the Nepali “paisley” (paankhi) curve.

Requirement: must work as (a) a physical engraving/motif on metal, (b) a 2D logo mark, (c) a packaging pattern, (d) a social media avatar at 40px.

Deliverable: 3–5 motif concepts from a designer/illustrator, tested on 2–3 physical prototypes before locking.

2.3 Trust & Transparency Pillar

Purity fraud (cadmium-mixed silver, undisclosed alloy content) is a documented, real pain point in the Nepali market. This is a genuine differentiation angle and should be the second pillar of every piece of marketing, not a footnote.

Public commitment: cadmium-free, disclosed alloy composition on every listing and receipt.

Phase 1: a printed purity/certificate card with lab-test batch reference number — no blockchain needed.

Phase 2+: NFC tag on packaging linking to a web page with the piece's material origin, karigar (artisan) credit, and batch certificate — a simple database-backed page, not a blockchain product passport.

2.4 Brand Voice

Confident, warm, specific — never generic luxury-speak (“indulge,” “timeless elegance”).

Leads with craft and provenance: name the karigar, name the material, name the motif's meaning.

Bilingual by default: Nepali-first tone for local buyers, English for diaspora/global.

3.3 Typography

Headings: a refined serif (e.g. Playfair Display, Canela, or a licensed equivalent) for warmth and craft association.

Body/UI: a clean grotesk (e.g. Inter, Suisse) for readability on mobile — most Nepali traffic will be mobile-first.

3.4 Photography Style

Warm ivory/neutral backdrops, natural light emulation, macro shots showing texture and motif detail.

Every collection includes at least one shoot on a real Nepali model in a real setting (temple courtyard, Kathmandu Valley, a wedding context) — not generic AI-model renders. AI-generated imagery, if used at all, is a Phase 2+ cost-saving supplement for catalogue filler shots only, never for hero/campaign imagery.

4.3 Core Page Requirements

Home

Hero: single strongest campaign image, one clear CTA (Shop the Collection).

Motif introduction strip — 3–4 pieces shown with the motif detail zoomed in.

Trust strip: cadmium-free badge, purity certificate mention, karigar credit line.

Social proof: Instagram feed embed or UGC strip.

Product Detail Page (PDP)

High-res image set (min. 6 angles) + one macro shot of the motif detail.

Spec block: metal, purity (with certificate reference number), weight, gemstone info if applicable.

Karigar credit (“Crafted by [name/atelier] in [location]”) — reinforces provenance story.

Sizing guide (rings) and a simple size-comparison photo (coin/finger reference) in lieu of AR in Phase 1.

WhatsApp “Ask about this piece” button — Nepali buyers convert heavily through chat; don't force everything through a Western-style cart flow.

Purity & Provenance Page

Explains lab-testing process, cadmium-free commitment, and how to verify a specific piece's certificate number.

Phase 1: static explanation + manual certificate lookup (email/WhatsApp request).

Phase 2: searchable certificate number field on this page, backed by a simple database — not blockchain.

Checkout

Guest checkout mandatory — do not force account creation.

Local payment methods: eSewa, Khalti, Fonepay, plus COD (cash on delivery) since COD is still dominant in Nepali e-commerce and RTO (return-to-origin) risk needs a defined policy — e.g. partial deposit for COD orders above a set value.

Clear, itemised delivery timeline by zone (Kathmandu Valley vs. outside valley vs. diaspora/international).

4.5 Phase 2 (Post-revenue) Feature Set

NFC tag + database-backed provenance lookup (real DPP-lite, no blockchain).

Basic AR try-on for rings/earrings via a mid-market tool (evaluate cost/SKU before committing — verify current vendor pricing directly rather than assuming; Threekit-class tools are enterprise-priced and only justified once catalogue and revenue support the per-SKU 3D-scanning cost).

Email/SMS lifecycle marketing (abandoned cart, restock alerts, gifting-occasion reminders around Dashain/Tihar/wedding season).

Loyalty / referral program (jewelry is gift-driven and high-referral by nature).

4.6 Phase 3 (Scale) Feature Set

Full blockchain-backed Digital Product Passport — only once you have the volume and international buyer base (diaspora, export) where third-party verification actually matters commercially.

AI-assisted photorealistic catalogue renders for filler/variant SKUs (never for hero campaign imagery).

In-house or dedicated CAD + 3D-print production line — once order volume justifies owning this instead of outsourcing to existing karigars/manufacturers.

International shipping/customs-compliant checkout for diaspora markets (Gulf, US, Australia, UK).

4.7 Non-Functional Requirements

Mobile-first performance: sub-3-second load on 4G, since most Nepali mobile traffic is not on premium networks.

Nepali + English bilingual site from day one.

PCI-compliant payment handling via chosen gateway/aggregator; do not build custom payment handling.

Basic SEO fundamentals (structured data on PDPs, sitemap, fast core web vitals) — organic search is a free CAC channel this market under-uses.

5. Operations Stack

5.1 Inventory & Showroom — DipArtha

Use DipArtha (Autonomous Technology / Swordfish Technology) for day-to-day inventory, dual weight tracking (grams/tola), barcode billing, and karigar order tracking. It is IRD-recognised and already adopted by 200+ Kathmandu jewelers — low integration risk. Cost: roughly NPR 30,000–90,000/year depending on package, plus annual renewal.

5.2 Order-to-Delivery Flow

Order placed on website (prepaid via eSewa/Khalti/Fonepay, or COD with deposit above threshold) → order synced to DipArtha → pick/pack from showroom stock or routed to karigar for made-to-order pieces → quality check against certificate spec → dispatch via courier partner → delivery confirmation → post-delivery review/referral prompt.

Custom/bridal orders route to a manual sales consultation (call/WhatsApp/showroom visit) rather than the standard checkout — high-touch, high-AOV, needs a human.

6.2 Launch Campaign Concept

“Not by weight. By design.” — a campaign built around the two pillars: the signature motif (design identity) and the purity guarantee (trust identity). Launch content directly contrasts old-model buying (priced by tola, no design identity, no purity guarantee) against the new model, without naming competitors.

Hero video: karigar hands crafting the signature motif, cut with a customer wearing the finished piece.

Launch collection sized deliberately small (8–12 SKUs) to keep photography, DipArtha setup, and cash-tied-up-in-inventory manageable.

Seed via micro-influencers before paid spend — validate messaging cheaply before scaling ad budget.

Occasion-led: Dashain, Tihar, wedding season, engagement gifting guides.

6.5 CAC / LTV Discipline

Set a maximum acceptable CAC per SKU tier before spending (e.g. entry-tier silver piece vs. bridal-tier gold piece will tolerate very different CAC).

Track repeat purchase rate and referral rate explicitly — jewelry is gift-driven and high-referral; a referral program should be treated as a core growth channel, not an afterthought.

Do not scale paid spend past Phase 1 budget until at least one full sales cycle of real CAC/AOV/repeat-rate data exists.

8. Risks & Open Questions

COD/RTO risk: Nepali e-commerce still leans COD-heavy; needs a deposit or verification policy for high-value items to avoid return-to-origin losses eating margin.

Inventory capital lock-up: jewelry ties up more cash per SKU than typical DTC goods — launch SKU count should stay deliberately small until sell-through data exists.

NRB / import-export regulatory constraints on gold and silver may affect sourcing and, later, diaspora/export shipping — confirm current rules before committing to Phase 3 export plans.

Vendor claims (AR conversion lift, AI rendering tools) in this space are often generic marketing stats, not Nepal-specific — re-validate actual vendor pricing and terms at the time of Phase 2/3 evaluation rather than assuming today's figures hold.

Karigar capacity: confirm how many artisans can realistically support the launch collection's production timeline before finalising launch SKU count.
